⚗️ Chemical Reactions and Equations — Class 10

Types of reactions, balancing equations, oxidation-reduction, and corrosion

1. Chemical Reactions

📖 What is a Chemical Reaction?

A chemical reaction is a process in which substances (reactants) are converted into new substances (products) with different properties. During a chemical reaction, bonds in reactants break and new bonds form in products.

⚡ Signs of a Chemical Reaction

• Change in colour (e.g., copper turns black when heated in air)

• Change in state (e.g., liquid → gas, solid → liquid)

• Evolution of gas (e.g., bubbles when vinegar + baking soda)

• Formation of precipitate (insoluble solid formed in solution)

• Change in temperature (heat absorbed or released)

• Change in smell (new odour produced)

2. Chemical Equations

📖 Writing Chemical Equations

A chemical equation is a symbolic representation of a chemical reaction.

Format: Reactants → Products

Example: Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium Oxide

Word equation: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO

States: (s) = solid, (l) = liquid, (g) = gas, (aq) = aqueous (dissolved in water)

⚡ Balancing Chemical Equations

A balanced equation obeys the Law of Conservation of Mass: atoms of each element must be equal on both sides.

Method: Hit and Trial

Example: H₂ + O₂ → H₂O

Count: Left: 2H, 2O  |  Right: 2H, 1O → Oxygen not balanced

Balanced: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O ✓ (4H, 2O on both sides)

💡 Example: Balance Fe + H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂

Start with Fe₃O₄ (most complex): needs 3Fe, 4O on left

3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂

Count: Left: 3Fe, 4O, 8H  |  Right: 3Fe, 4O, 2H → H not balanced

Balanced: 3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂

Verify: 3Fe, 4O, 8H on both sides ✓

3. Types of Chemical Reactions

3.1 Combination Reaction

📖 Definition

Two or more substances combine to form a single product: A + B → AB

  • Burning of coal: C + O₂ → CO₂
  • Formation of water: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
  • Slaking of lime: CaO + H₂O → Ca(OH)₂ (exothermic — generates heat)
  • Quick lime + water: Heat is released, used in hand warmers

3.2 Decomposition Reaction

📖 Definition

A single compound breaks into two or more substances: AB → A + B

Requires energy input: heat (thermal), electricity (electrolysis), or light (photolysis)

  • Thermal decomposition of calcium carbonate: CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ (used in cement industry)
  • Electrolysis of water: 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂ (electricity used)
  • Decomposition of silver chloride by light: 2AgCl → 2Ag + Cl₂ (photolysis — used in photography)

3.3 Displacement Reaction

📖 Definition

A more reactive element displaces a less reactive element from its compound: A + BC → AC + B

  • Zn + CuSO₄ → ZnSO₄ + Cu (Zinc displaces Copper — Zn more reactive)
  • Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu (Iron displaces Copper)
  • Cu + FeSO₄ → No reaction (Cu less reactive than Fe)

3.4 Double Displacement (Precipitation) Reaction

📖 Definition

Two compounds exchange ions to form two new compounds. Often one product is a precipitate (insoluble solid): AB + CD → AD + CB

  • Na₂SO₄ + BaCl₂ → BaSO₄↓ + 2NaCl (white precipitate of BaSO₄ forms)
  • NaOH + HCl → NaCl + H₂O (neutralisation reaction)
  • AgNO₃ + NaCl → AgCl↓ + NaNO₃ (white precipitate of AgCl)

3.5 Oxidation and Reduction (Redox)

📖 Definitions

Oxidation: Gain of oxygen OR loss of hydrogen OR loss of electrons

Reduction: Loss of oxygen OR gain of hydrogen OR gain of electrons

Redox reaction: Oxidation and reduction occur simultaneously

💡 Example: CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O

CuO loses oxygen → CuO is reduced (it's an oxidising agent)

H₂ gains oxygen → H₂ is oxidised (it's a reducing agent)

Remember: OIL RIG — Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain (of electrons)

4. Effects of Oxidation in Daily Life

4.1 Corrosion

📖 What is Corrosion?

Corrosion is the slow deterioration of a metal due to chemical reactions with moisture and air in the environment.

• Rusting of iron: 4Fe + 3O₂ + xH₂O → 2Fe₂O₃.xH₂O (rust, reddish-brown)

• Blackening of silver: 2Ag + H₂S → Ag₂S + H₂ (tarnishing)

• Greenish layer on copper: Cu + CO₂ + H₂O → basic copper carbonate

4.2 Rancidity

📖 What is Rancidity?

Rancidity is the oxidation of oils and fats in food, producing an unpleasant smell and taste.

Prevention:

• Store food in airtight containers

• Add antioxidants (BHA, BHT) — used in chips, fried snacks

• Refrigerate food (low temperature slows oxidation)

• Replace air with nitrogen gas (nitrogen flushing in packaged food)

🔑 Summary of All Reaction Types

  • Combination: A + B → AB (two become one)
  • Decomposition: AB → A + B (one splits into many)
  • Displacement: A + BC → AC + B (one element displaces another)
  • Double Displacement: AB + CD → AD + CB (exchange of partners)
  • Redox: Simultaneous oxidation + reduction (always together)
