🫁 Breathing and Exchange of Gases

1. Respiratory Organs

Mechanisms of breathing vary among different groups. Lower invertebrates (sponges, flatworms) use simple diffusion. Earthworms use moist cuticle. Insects use tracheal tubes. Aquatic arthropods/fishes use gills. Terrestrial forms use lungs.

2. Human Respiratory System

Nostrils → Pharynx → Larynx (sound box) → Trachea → Bronchi → Bronchioles → Alveoli.

The alveoli and their ducts form the respiratory or exchange part of the respiratory system. Lungs are covered by double-layered pleura.

3. Mechanism of Breathing

Breathing involves two stages: Inspiration (drawing in air) and Expiration (releasing air). Driven by pressure gradients generated by the diaphragm and external/internal intercostal muscles.

Respiratory Volumes and Capacities

Tidal Volume (TV): Vol. of air inspired/expired during normal respiration (~500 mL).

Residual Volume (RV): Air remaining in lungs after forcible expiration (~1200 mL).

Vital Capacity (VC): Maximum volume of air a person can breathe in after a forced expiration (or vice versa).

4. Exchange and Transport of Gases

Exchange of O₂ and CO₂ occurs between alveoli and blood, and blood and tissues via simple diffusion based on pressure/concentration gradients (Partial Pressure: pO₂, pCO₂).

Transport Mechanisms

Oxygen: ~97% transported by RBCs binding reversibly to hemoglobin to form oxyhemoglobin. 3% dissolved in plasma.

Carbon dioxide: ~20-25% bound to hemoglobin (carbamino-hemoglobin). ~70% transported as bicarbonate ions (HCO₃⁻). ~7% dissolved in plasma.

5. Regulation and Disorders

Respiratory rhythm center in the medulla region of the brain is primarily responsible for regulation. It is highly sensitive to CO₂ and H⁺ concentrations, NOT oxygen.

Disorders: Asthma (inflammation of bronchi), Emphysema (alveolar walls damaged, due to smoking), Occupational respiratory disorders.
